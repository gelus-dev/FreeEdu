States of Matter

Learn States of Matter and examples ✅

Overview

Definition: States of matter refer to the distinct forms in which matter can exist. The most common states are solid, liquid, gas, and plasma. Matter in each state exhibits different physical properties like shape, volume, density, and compressibility.

Primary States of Matter:

1. Solid: Fixed shape and volume, particles tightly packed, strong intermolecular forces.

2. Liquid: Fixed volume but takes the shape of the container, particles less tightly packed, moderate intermolecular forces.

3. Gas: No fixed shape or volume, particles far apart, weak intermolecular forces, highly compressible.

4. Plasma: Ionized gas, conducts electricity, particles highly energetic, found in stars and lightning.

Secondary/Advanced Concepts:

5. Bose-Einstein Condensate (BEC): Ultra-cold state where particles occupy the same quantum state.

6. Fermionic Condensate: Similar to BEC but formed with fermions at near absolute zero.

Phase Changes:

• Melting: Solid → Liquid, e.g., Ice → Water
• Freezing: Liquid → Solid, e.g., Water → Ice
• Vaporization: Liquid → Gas, e.g., Water → Steam
• Condensation: Gas → Liquid, e.g., Steam → Water
• Sublimation: Solid → Gas, e.g., Dry Ice → CO₂ gas
• Deposition: Gas → Solid, e.g., Frost formation

High School Level Formulas:

• Ideal Gas Law: \( PV = nRT \)
• Density: \( \rho = \frac{m}{V} \)
• Diffusion Rate (Graham’s Law): \( \frac{r_1}{r_2} = \sqrt{\frac{M_2}{M_1}} \)
• Specific Heat: \( Q = mc\Delta T \)
• Latent Heat: \( Q = mL \)

Examples

Example 1: Identifying State

Given: Water at 25°C
Find: State of matter
Solution: At 25°C, water is in liquid state.

Example 2: Using Ideal Gas Law

Given: 2 moles of gas, T = 300 K, V = 10 L
Find: Pressure P
Solution: \( P = \frac{nRT}{V} = \frac{2*0.0821*300}{10} = 4.926 \text{ atm} \)

Example 3: Density Calculation

Given: Mass = 50 g, Volume = 20 cm³
Find: Density
Solution: \( \rho = \frac{m}{V} = \frac{50}{20} = 2.5 \text{ g/cm³} \)

Example 4: Sublimation

Given: Dry ice at -78.5°C
Find: Phase change
Solution: Dry ice sublimates directly from solid to CO₂ gas.
