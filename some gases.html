Some Gases

Learn Some Gases and examples ✅

Overview

Definition: Gases are one of the fundamental states of matter characterized by particles that are widely separated, move freely, and have no definite shape or volume. They completely fill their containers and can be compressed easily.

General Properties of Gases:

1. Physical Properties: No definite shape or volume, highly compressible, low density

2. Kinetic Theory: Particles in constant random motion, negligible intermolecular forces

3. Gas Laws: Boyle's Law (P ∝ 1/V), Charles's Law (V ∝ T), Gay-Lussac's Law (P ∝ T)

4. Ideal Gas Equation: PV = nRT (where P=pressure, V=volume, n=moles, R=gas constant, T=temperature)

5. Avogadro's Law: Equal volumes of gases at same temperature and pressure contain equal number of molecules

Gas Collection Methods:

• Displacement of Water: For gases slightly soluble in water (O₂, H₂)
• Downward Displacement of Air: For gases denser than air (CO₂, Cl₂)
• Upward Displacement of Air: For gases lighter than air (NH₃, H₂)

Standard Conditions:

• STP: 273K (0°C), 1 atm pressure, 22.4L molar volume
• SATP: 298K (25°C), 1 bar pressure, 24.5L molar volume

Carbon Dioxide (CO₂)

Laboratory Preparation:

Principle: Action of dilute acid on metal carbonates/bicarbonates

CaCO₃ + 2HCl → CaCl₂ + H₂O + CO₂↑
NaHCO₃ + HCl → NaCl + H₂O + CO₂↑

Apparatus: Kipp's apparatus or simple gas generation setup

Collection: Downward displacement of air (denser than air)

Physical Properties:

• Colourless, odourless gas
• Density: 1.98 g/L at STP
• Soluble in water (1:1 ratio)
• Heavier than air
• Can be liquefied under pressure

Chemical Properties:

• Non-combustible and non-supporter of combustion
• Turns lime water milky
• Forms carbonic acid with water
• Acidic oxide

CO₂ + Ca(OH)₂ → CaCO₃↓ + H₂O (lime water test)
CO₂ + H₂O → H₂CO₃ (carbonic acid)

Important Reactions:

CO₂ + 2Mg → 2MgO + C (reduction by metals)
CO₂ + C → 2CO (at high temperature)
CO₂ + 2NH₃ → (NH₂)₂CO + H₂O (urea formation)

Ammonia (NH₃)

Laboratory Preparation:

Principle: Heating ammonium salt with strong alkali

NH₄Cl + NaOH → NaCl + H₂O + NH₃↑
2NH₄Cl + Ca(OH)₂ → CaCl₂ + 2H₂O + 2NH₃↑

Collection: Upward displacement of air (lighter than air)

Drying Agent: Quick lime (CaO) - not H₂SO₄ as NH₃ is basic

Physical Properties:

• Colourless gas with pungent smell
• Density: 0.771 g/L at STP
• Highly soluble in water (1:700 ratio)
• Lighter than air
• Easily liquefied

Chemical Properties:

• Basic in nature
• Forms complex compounds
• Reducing agent
• Forms ammonium salts with acids

NH₃ + H₂O → NH₄OH (ammonium hydroxide)
NH₃ + HCl → NH₄Cl (white fumes test)

Important Reactions:

4NH₃ + 5O₂ → 4NO + 6H₂O (catalytic oxidation)
2NH₃ + 3CuO → 3Cu + N₂ + 3H₂O (reduction)
NH₃ + 3Cl₂ → NCl₃ + 3HCl (with chlorine)

Hydrogen (H₂)

Laboratory Preparation:

Principle: Action of dilute acid on active metals

Zn + H₂SO₄ → ZnSO₄ + H₂↑
Zn + 2HCl → ZnCl₂ + H₂↑

Collection: Downward displacement of water or upward displacement of air

Precaution: Test for purity before lighting (pop sound test)

Physical Properties:

• Colourless, odourless, tasteless gas
• Density: 0.089 g/L at STP (lightest gas)
• Slightly soluble in water
• Diffuses rapidly
• Non-toxic

Chemical Properties:

• Combustible but not supporter of combustion
• Reducing agent
• Forms water on combustion
• Neutral to litmus

2H₂ + O₂ → 2H₂O (combustion)
H₂ + Cl₂ → 2HCl (synthesis)

Important Reactions:

H₂ + CuO → Cu + H₂O (reduction of metal oxides)
3H₂ + N₂ → 2NH₃ (Haber process)
H₂ + C₂H₄ → C₂H₆ (hydrogenation)

Oxygen (O₂)

Laboratory Preparation:

Principle: Thermal decomposition of oxygen-rich compounds

2KClO₃ → 2KCl + 3O₂↑ (with MnO₂ catalyst)
2H₂O₂ → 2H₂O + O₂↑ (with MnO₂ catalyst)
2KMnO₄ → K₂MnO₄ + MnO₂ + O₂↑

Collection: Downward displacement of water

Physical Properties:

• Colourless, odourless, tasteless gas
• Density: 1.429 g/L at STP
• Slightly soluble in water
• Slightly heavier than air
• Paramagnetic

Chemical Properties:

• Supporter of combustion
• Oxidizing agent
• Forms oxides with most elements
• Essential for respiration

S + O₂ → SO₂ (combustion of sulfur)
4Fe + 3O₂ → 2Fe₂O₃ (rusting)

Important Reactions:

2Mg + O₂ → 2MgO (bright white flame)
C + O₂ → CO₂ (complete combustion)
2C + O₂ → 2CO (incomplete combustion)

Nitrogen (N₂)

Laboratory Preparation:

Principle: Removal of oxygen from air or decomposition of compounds

NaNO₂ + NH₄Cl → NaCl + 2H₂O + N₂↑
(NH₄)₂Cr₂O₇ → Cr₂O₃ + N₂↑ + 4H₂O

From Air: Pass air over heated copper to remove oxygen

Collection: Downward displacement of water

Physical Properties:

• Colourless, odourless, tasteless gas
• Density: 1.25 g/L at STP
• Slightly soluble in water
• Slightly lighter than air
• Chemically inert

Chemical Properties:

• Very stable (triple bond)
• Non-combustible and non-supporter
• Inert at room temperature
• Forms compounds at high temperature/pressure

N₂ + 3H₂ ⇌ 2NH₃ (Haber process - high T, P, catalyst)
N₂ + O₂ → 2NO (at very high temperature)

Important Reactions:

N₂ + 3Mg → Mg₃N₂ (with burning magnesium)
N₂ + 6Li → 2Li₃N (with lithium)
N₂ + CaC₂ → CaCN₂ + C (calcium cyanamide process)

Solved Examples

Example 1: Gas Volume Calculation

Problem: Calculate the volume of CO₂ produced when 10g of CaCO₃ reacts with excess HCl at STP.
Given: CaCO₃ + 2HCl → CaCl₂ + H₂O + CO₂↑
Solution:
Molar mass of CaCO₃ = 100 g/mol
Moles of CaCO₃ = 10/100 = 0.1 mol
From equation: 1 mol CaCO₃ produces 1 mol CO₂
Volume of CO₂ at STP = 0.1 × 22.4 = 2.24 L

Example 2: Gas Collection Method

Problem: Why is NH₃ collected by upward displacement of air?
Solution:
• NH₃ density = 0.771 g/L
• Air density = 1.29 g/L
• Since NH₃ is lighter than air, it rises up
• Also, NH₃ is highly soluble in water, so water displacement is not suitable

Example 3: Chemical Test

Problem: How do you distinguish between CO₂ and SO₂?
Solution:
• Both turn lime water milky initially
• CO₂: Milkiness persists
• SO₂: Milkiness disappears on excess gas (forms bisulfite)
• SO₂ has pungent smell, CO₂ is odourless
• SO₂ decolorizes KMnO₄, CO₂ doesn't

Example 4: Catalytic Process

Problem: Why is MnO₂ used in preparation of O₂ from KClO₃?
Solution:
• MnO₂ acts as catalyst - speeds up decomposition
• Lowers activation energy for the reaction
• Without catalyst: 2KClO₃ → 2KCl + 3O₂ (at 400°C)
• With MnO₂: Same reaction occurs at 270°C
• MnO₂ remains unchanged after reaction
