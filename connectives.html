Connectives

Learn Connectives and Practice Questions ✅

Overview

Definition: Connectives are words or phrases that link clauses, sentences, or paragraphs to show relationships such as addition, contrast, cause-effect, time, or condition. They ensure smooth flow and coherence in writing or speech.

Key Concepts :

1. Types of Connectives:
• Addition – and, also, moreover, furthermore
• Contrast – but, however, although, nevertheless
• Cause/Effect – because, so, therefore, as a result
• Time – when, before, after, while, since
• Condition – if, unless, provided that, in case
• Comparison – than, as…as, like
• Purpose – so that, in order that
• Concession – though, although, even though
• Result – consequently, hence, thus

2. Usage Rules:
• Connectives must appropriately match the relationship intended.
• They can appear at the beginning, middle, or end of a sentence depending on emphasis.
• Use punctuation correctly when connectives start a sentence (e.g., comma after introductory connectives like "However, ...").

3. Exceptions & Special Cases:
• “And” cannot always join unrelated ideas; context must be logical.
• “But” should not start a sentence in formal writing unless stylistically intended.
• “Although” introduces a subordinate clause and cannot stand alone.
• Some connectives (like “so”) can be conjunction or adverb depending on position.
• Avoid double connectives that are redundant (e.g., “because since” is incorrect).
• In negative sentences, some connectives may require inversion or different structure (e.g., “Not only… but also…”).
